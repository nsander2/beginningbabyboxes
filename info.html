More Information

Listed below are a collection of educational resources for parents that cover a variety of newborn topics.

What is (SIDS)

SIDS stands for sudden infant death syndrome. According to the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) “more than 3,500 babies in the U.S die suddenly or unexpectedly every year while sleeping”. The National Institute of Child Health (NIH) states that “SIDS is the leady cause of death in babies from 1 month to 1 year of age”. This guide was produced to gather essential and accurate information from medical professionals to help guide parents of newborn babies and reduce the risk of all sleep-related infant deaths.




Sleeping Safe on their Backs

> According to the AAP and NIH, “babies who sleep on their backs are much less likely to die of SIDS than babies who sleep on their stomachs or sides.” The AAP addresses a parent’s natural worry of their baby choking while laying on their back and states, “a baby’s airway anatomy and the gag reflex will keep choking from happening” “Even babies with gastroesophageal reflux (GERD) should sleep on their backs.”

> Some babies will roll onto their stomachs. You should always place your baby to sleep on the back, but if your baby is comfortable moving both ways (back to tummy, tummy to back), then you do not have to return your baby to the back. However, be sure that there are no blankets, pillows, stuffed toys, or bumper pads around your baby so that your baby does not roll into any of those items, which could block airflow. – AAP

> If your baby falls asleep in a car seat, stroller, swing, infant carrier, or sling, you should move him or her to a firm sleep surface on his or her back as soon as possible. – AAP

Sleeping Surfaces Matter

> The AAP and NIH recommend that babies sleep on a firm sleeping surface, such as a crib, bassinet, portable crib, or play yard that meets the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC)* standards.

> * For information on crib safety, contact the Consumer Product Safety Commission at 1-800-638-2772 or http://www.cpsc.gov

> The AAP states that “nothing else should be in the crib except for the baby."a firm surface is a hard surface; it should not indent when the baby is lying on it.”

> The NIH states, “Do not use a car seat, carrier, swing, or similar product as baby’s everyday sleep area.” And “Never place baby to sleep on soft surfaces, such as on a couch or sofa, pillows, quilts, sheepskins, or blankets.”


Room Sharing

> The AAP recommends room sharing with your newborn because they claim that “it can decrease the risk of SIDS by as much as 50% and is much safer than bed-sharing.”

> Both the AAP and NIH recommend that “your baby should not sleep in an adult bed, on a couch, or a chair alone, with you, or with anyone else. If you bring a baby into your bed to feed, make sure to put him or her back in a separate sleep area when finished.”


Keep Sleeping Space Object Free

> Having an object in a baby sleeping area increases the risk of entrapment, suffocation, or strangulation.

> Avoid putting pillows, blankets, quilts, sheepskins, comforters, toys, bumper pads, or similar products that attach to crib slats or sides in a baby sleeping area. The AAP suggests if you are worried that your baby will get cold, you can use infant sleeping clothing such as a wearable blanket. Another general tip from the AAP is that “your baby should be dressed in only one layer more than you are wearing.”
